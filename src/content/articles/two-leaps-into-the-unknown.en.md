Moving to a new city and joining a founding team are two leaps into the unknown that are more similar than they seem.

&nbsp;

A year ago, my life was pretty predictable.

I was working at a company of over 80 people, with a great team, clear processes, and a steady pace. I had daily meetings, weekly tasks, defined goals. Every week I knew exactly what I had to do.

And yet, something was missing.

It wasn’t a lack of technical challenge. It wasn’t the team either.
It was something harder to explain: the feeling that I wasn’t building something that could truly change how the world works.

One night, like many others, I was scrolling through Twitter.
That’s when I saw a post by Tom Piaggio, CTO of Autonoma.

He was talking about what they were building.

The problem immediately resonated with me. Not because it was new, but because I had run into it many times before: end-to-end testing.

It was always a source of friction.
AI was starting to help, but there still wasn’t anything that consistently accelerated the pace of work.

I didn’t overthink it.

I thought: **this could be big.**

It wasn’t the first time I felt that. I had already been part of a founding team before, at Sirvana (now Melian). I knew what it meant: uncertainty, intensity, constant decision-making.

And still, I chose to do it again.

&nbsp;

## A double leap

The process moved quickly. A series of interviews over the span of two weeks.

The night they told me they wanted me to join, I felt something I hadn’t felt in a while.

It wasn’t just excitement.

It was more like a sense of alignment with myself.
Like something inside me was being switched back on.

But this time, there was something different.

It wasn’t just a job change.
It also meant moving to *Buenos Aires*, leaving the city where I was born, and leaving behind an entire world I lived in every day: family, friends, routines, my home.

It was a double leap.

&nbsp;

## Five people and a half-built product

![Autonoma Office](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/Gm7VkYPWIAAAXrn?format=jpg&name=large "max-height=28rem")

A week after getting the offer, I took a bus to meet the team.

The office was on *Castañeda* street, in *Belgrano*.

One room.
A large table.
Two coffee machines.
A whiteboard.

We were five people.

The product existed, but it was far from being a finished product.

There was a screen we called **Creation**.
It opened a browser transmitted in real time, where every interaction the user made was saved as a step. Those steps were later replayed and validated conditions.

In essence, it was a test.
But without code.

It worked.
Kind of.

If I'm being honest, almost everything was still raw at that point:

the user experience, the interface, the infrastructure, the API, the business model.

We didn’t really have customers yet.
We had design partners, paying between $199 and $499, helping us shape something we didn't fully understand yet.

There was no certainty.

There was something more subtle.

A shared sense that something might happen.

&nbsp;

## Making it feel right

My first week was simple.

I added breadcrumbs.

It wasn’t a big feature, but it started to bring order to something that didn’t have much of it yet.

The core of the product was **Creation**, and it was probably the most complex part: a mix of frontend, streaming, browser, execution engine, and networking that technically worked, but was held together with duct tape.

We tried building a feature called **Checkpoint**, something like a save point in a game: replay a test up to a certain step, modify it from there, and continue.

It sounded simple.

It took us three weeks.

And it didn’t work the way we wanted.

That’s when something clicked for me:

> Building something simple doesn’t mean it’s easy. More often than not, it’s the opposite.

We worked every day connected on Meet.

We had a massive, endless document filled with tasks, bugs, ideas, and features.

Everything lived in the same place.

But something became clear:

if we wanted this to grow, it wasn’t enough for it to just work.

> It had to feel right.

That’s where I leaned in.

We didn’t do a massive redesign.
There was no big refactor.

Instead, we made constant changes, almost invisible day to day.

The layout.
The navigation.
The pages.
The interactions.

Every decision aimed at the same thing:

making the product feel natural.

So that you wouldn’t have to think before clicking.
So that you’d understand what would happen without hesitation.

Over time, those changes accumulated.

And looking back, the transformation was massive.

We ended up reworking almost everything.

The creation screen, test views, folders, dashboard, analytics, integrations, settings.
Even how we presented ourselves publicly.

### Rebuilding Creation

One of the biggest changes was rebuilding **Creation**.

It took months.

But once it started working properly, something interesting happened:

it didn’t just unblock the product — it revealed over 500 bugs we hadn’t even seen before.

Not because they weren’t there.
But because users couldn't get that far.

At the same time, we started showing what we were building.

We recorded demos.
We shared progress.
We built in public.

That created something we didn't fully anticipate:

constant feedback, visibility, and a healthy pressure to improve.

&nbsp;

### The Vercel stretch

![Vercel Installations Graph](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/G6nylbkWEAAKwvF?format=jpg&name=large)

Over time, things started happening.

Customers like NaranjaX, Ualá, Mercor, Superhuman.
The team growing.
A trip to *São Paulo*.

And in the middle of it all, one of the most intense periods of the year:

integrating with **Vercel**.

Those were long weeks.

Hours and hours on Meet with the entire team, solving problems in real time.

There wasn’t much room to overthink.
It was iterate, test, break, and try again.

The integration shipped.

And today, it’s the most installed integration across the entire **Vercel Marketplace**.
And the only one built in Argentina.

&nbsp;

## When growth isn’t enough

By that point, the product had changed completely.

We had grown **10x in ARR**.
Crossed **$500,000**.

Everything pointed in the right direction.

And still, something felt off.

When we started comparing what a human could do versus what an agent, a system powered by AI capable of acting autonomously, could do, the gap was massive.

The product validated something important: there was real value.

But it wasn’t enough for the change we wanted to create, especially in a world where companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google were pushing the limits every day.

And that’s when one of the hardest decisions appears:

> Realizing that what you built… isn’t the final thing.

We decided to start over.

Not because what we had built was wrong.
But because it wasn’t what we wanted to build long term.

Letting go of something that works isn’t easy.

There’s anxiety.
Uncertainty.
Moments where it feels like you’re throwing away months of work.

But as the weeks pass, something else starts to emerge:

the feeling that you’re getting closer to what’s right.

&nbsp;

## The second leap

This year had several leaps into the unknown.

Moving to a new city was one.
Starting something from scratch again was another.

But there’s something I learned:

no leap is ever the same as the previous one.

Each time you do it, you bring something with you.

Better judgment.
Stronger intuition.
More tolerance for chaos.

It doesn’t make it easier.
But it makes it different.

Today we’re more.

We grew from five to twelve people.
We work with companies that felt out of reach a year ago.

And more importantly, we’re building something we still don’t fully know how it will end.

And that’s okay.

The past year confirmed something I’ve been learning over and over again:

the most important things you build don’t come from comfort.

They come from stepping back into the mud.

From accepting that you don’t have all the answers.
From allowing yourself to start again.

And maybe the most interesting part is this:

after everything that happened this past year…

this time, it feels different.
